America’s Top Colleges

Five hundred colleges that shine
Edited by Emma Whitford and Janet Novack

MIT takes the #1 spot on Forbes’ annual list, which showcases 500 colleges that produce successful, high-earning and influential graduates from all economic backgrounds, with less student debt. Despite President Trump’s political assault, the eight Ivies all placed in the top 20, as did three University of California campuses.

To say it’s been a rough year for American colleges would be an understatement. Over the past eight months, President Donald Trump, his administration and Congressional Republicans have waged financial and cultural war on higher education—freezing research funds, punishing efforts at campus diversity, constricting the flow of foreign students, raising the tax on some college endowments and curbing the generosity of student loan programs. Despite this, American colleges are still delivering gold-standard undergraduate education. This year, the Massachusetts Institute of Technology reclaims the #1 spot on Forbes’ list of the best colleges in the United States, acing all 14 metrics we use to determine which 500 schools offer the best educational, financial and career outcomes for all students. Among its standout numbers: Graduates earn a median salary of $110,200 just three years after graduating and nearly all students (99.8%) return to MIT after their freshman year. Also in our top 10: “Little Ivy” Williams College in Massachusetts (#7), research powerhouse Johns Hopkins University (#8) and perennial public college stand-out University of California-Berkeley (#5). Five of the eight Ivy League schools are in the top 10 and all made the top 20.
Cost and how well a school fits a student (academically and otherwise), should remain the prime factors as families consider college choices. But the current turmoil could also affect undergraduates’ experiences, college counselors warn. Fortunately, they’ve also shared with Forbes tips for navigating the college search process amid the mayhem.
The 500 schools below are ranked based exclusively on outcomes for undergraduates, but it’s worth noting that American graduate education is primed for a huge shakeup as Congress caps how much graduate students can borrow, and the Trump administration curbs research spending and student visas. Forbes identified 25 private universities that are especially vulnerable.
As always, Forbes has looked far beyond the Ivy League to identify the best schools. Earlier this year, we released our second annual New Ivies list, which honors 10 public and 10 private colleges that stand out to employers now. If a bustling public university is your target, take a look at the top 25 in the U.S., and if a small campus is more your speed, we’ve listed 50 of the best colleges with fewer than 4,000 undergraduates. We’ve also identified the 25 colleges (both public and private) that offer the best return on investment and 25 private schools that slash their price tags by half or more with generous financial aid. Methodology
We ranked U.S. colleges and universities based on return on investment, average student debt levels and outcomes for their graduates. Schools ranked highly on our list if their students returned after their first year, graduated on time, secured high salaries after graduation and left college with little student debt. We incorporated outcomes for low-income students in two measures, looking closely at six-year graduation rates and return-on-investment measures for recipients of Pell grants (federal aid reserved for students with the greatest financial need). We also rewarded colleges that enrolled a greater percentage of low-income students. For a complete explanation of our methodology, click here.
